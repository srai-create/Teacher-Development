Module 1

Behaviour for Learning

Creating a calm, focused classroom environment where learning can flourish

Key Learning

Effective behaviour management is the foundation of great teaching. Without a calm, orderly environment, even the best-planned lessons will struggle to have impact. Research consistently shows that teachers who establish clear expectations and routines from the outset spend less time managing behaviour and more time teaching.

  • Consistency is key: Students thrive when they know what to expect. Inconsistent responses to behaviour create uncertainty and anxiety.
  • Warm-strict approach: The most effective teachers combine genuine warmth and care with firm, non-negotiable boundaries.
  • Prevention over reaction: 90% of behaviour management happens before any misbehaviour occurs—through careful planning, clear systems, and positive relationships.
  • Least invasive intervention: Address low-level disruption with the smallest intervention possible to maintain lesson flow.

Remember: behaviour is a skill that can be taught, not just a problem to be managed. When students struggle behaviourally, they need teaching, practice, and feedback—just like any other skill.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Script and practise your attention signal. Choose a consistent phrase and action (e.g., "3-2-1, eyes on me" with hand raised). Rehearse in front of a mirror, focusing on calm authority and clear expectations. Use it consistently for one full week.
Task 2
During your next lesson, position yourself so you can see all students at all times. Move around the room during independent work, using proximity to prevent off-task behaviour before it escalates.
Task 3
Practise "positive framing" by converting three negative instructions into positive ones. Instead of "Don't call out," try "Put your hand up to share your answer." Record your reframed instructions and use them in class.

Module 2

Explaining & Modelling

Making complex ideas clear through structured explanations and worked examples

Key Learning

Clear explanations are the bridge between confusion and understanding. Expert teachers break down complex ideas into digestible chunks, use concrete examples, and think aloud to reveal the hidden processes that experts often take for granted.

  • Manage cognitive load: Working memory is limited. Present information in small, sequential steps to avoid overwhelming students.
  • Use dual coding: Combine verbal explanations with visual representations. When words and images work together, learning is stronger.
  • Think aloud: Make your expert thinking visible. Narrate your decision-making process when solving problems or analysing texts.
  • Concrete before abstract: Start with tangible examples before introducing abstract concepts. Anchor new learning to familiar contexts.

The goal is not to simplify content but to make the path to understanding clearer. Great explanations respect students' intelligence while acknowledging their current knowledge gaps.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Select a concept you'll teach this week. Write out your explanation in full, then cut it by 30%. Identify the essential steps and remove anything that doesn't directly support understanding.
Task 2
Create a live model for one key skill or process. Practise thinking aloud as you work through it, narrating every decision. Record yourself and listen back—are there any steps you skipped or assumed?
Task 3
Develop one strong analogy for an abstract concept in your subject. Test it with students and refine based on their responses. Does it illuminate or confuse?

Module 3

Questioning

Using strategic questions to check understanding, deepen thinking, and involve all learners

Key Learning

Questions are the engine of learning. Effective questioning helps teachers check understanding, pushes students to think more deeply, and ensures all learners are actively engaged—not just the confident few who raise their hands.

  • Cold call: Call on students regardless of whether their hand is raised. This keeps everyone alert and distributes participation equitably.
  • Wait time: Pause for 3-5 seconds after asking a question. This dramatically increases the quality and length of student responses.
  • Plan key questions: Your most important questions should be scripted in advance, not improvised. Think carefully about wording and sequence.
  • Probe and extend: Follow up initial answers with "Why?", "How do you know?", or "Can you give an example?" to push thinking deeper.

The purpose of questioning is not to catch students out, but to help them—and you—understand what they know, what they're confused about, and what they need to learn next.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Plan five key questions for your next lesson—the questions that will reveal whether students truly understand. Script the exact wording. After the lesson, note which questions worked well and why.
Task 2
Commit to using cold call for an entire lesson. Pose the question, pause, then name the student. Notice the difference in whole-class attention compared to hands-up questioning.
Task 3
Practise extending student responses by preparing three follow-up stems: "Why do you think that?", "Can you give me an example?", "What makes you say that?" Use each at least twice in your next lesson.

Module 4

Guided Practice

Scaffolding student practice with support before releasing to independence

Key Learning

Guided practice is the crucial bridge between teacher explanation and independent work. It's where students try out new skills with support, receive immediate feedback, and build the confidence needed to succeed alone.

  • Gradual release: Move from "I do" (teacher models) to "We do" (guided practice together) to "You do" (independent practice). Don't skip the middle step.
  • High success rate: During guided practice, students should be successful 80%+ of the time. If they're struggling significantly, you've moved too fast.
  • Active participation: Every student should be responding during guided practice—through mini-whiteboards, choral response, or written answers. Passive watching doesn't build skills.
  • Immediate feedback: Check responses as they happen and correct errors before they become embedded. The tighter the feedback loop, the better.

Rushing through guided practice to get to independent work is a false economy. Time invested here prevents confusion, frustration, and misconceptions later.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Plan a lesson using the "I do, We do, You do" structure explicitly. Time each phase and ensure guided practice takes at least as long as independent practice. Note how this affects student confidence.
Task 2
Use mini-whiteboards for a whole guided practice segment. Ask students to hold up boards simultaneously so you can scan all responses. Practise giving quick corrective feedback ("I can see some of us wrote X—remember that...").
Task 3
During your next guided practice, keep a mental note of success rate. If fewer than 80% of students are getting answers right, stop and reteach before continuing. Reflect on what this revealed about your initial explanation.

Module 5

Independent Practice

Designing purposeful practice that builds fluency and long-term retention

Key Learning

Independent practice is where learning is consolidated. It's the time when students apply what they've learned without scaffolding, building the fluency and automaticity that frees up working memory for higher-level thinking.

  • Purposeful design: Practice tasks should directly align with the lesson's learning objectives. Busy work doesn't equal learning.
  • Sufficient volume: Students need enough practice to develop fluency. One or two examples isn't enough—aim for deliberate repetition with slight variation.
  • Spaced practice: Don't just practise today's content. Interleave previous learning to strengthen long-term retention.
  • Active monitoring: Independent doesn't mean unmonitored. Circulate, observe, and provide targeted support where needed.

The goal is not just completion but quality practice. Students should be thinking hard about the right things, not racing through tasks to finish first.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Review a recent practice task you set. Ask: Does every question directly practise the skill I taught? Remove or replace any questions that don't align with the core learning objective.
Task 2
Design a practice task that interleaves today's learning with two concepts from previous weeks. Observe whether students find the older content harder and plan accordingly for future review.
Task 3
During independent practice, use a planned circulation route that takes you past every student at least twice. Keep brief notes on common errors you observe to address in the next lesson.

Module 6

Feedback

Providing feedback that moves learning forward efficiently and effectively

Key Learning

Feedback is only valuable if it leads to improved student work. The most impactful feedback is timely, specific, and actionable—and crucially, students must have the opportunity to act on it.

  • Focus on the work, not the person: Effective feedback addresses specific aspects of the work ("This paragraph needs a topic sentence") rather than the student ("You're not very good at paragraphs").
  • Less is more: One or two actionable points are better than a list of everything wrong. Prioritise the most impactful improvements.
  • Build in response time: Feedback without the opportunity to respond is largely wasted. Plan time for students to act on feedback immediately.
  • Verbal feedback is often better: A 30-second conversation can achieve more than 10 minutes of written comments—and is far more sustainable.

Rethink feedback as an ongoing dialogue about how to improve, not as a one-time judgement delivered after the fact.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Collect a set of student work and identify the single most impactful improvement point for each student. Write feedback that addresses only this one point, with a clear action for the student to take.
Task 2
Try "live marking" in your next lesson—circulate during independent practice and give brief verbal feedback to individuals. Aim for 8-10 quick interactions rather than lengthy conversations with 2-3 students.
Task 3
After providing feedback, dedicate the first 10 minutes of the next lesson to "DIRT" (Directed Improvement and Reflection Time). Students respond to feedback immediately while it's still fresh. Observe the quality of improvements.

Module 7

Assessment for Learning

Using assessment to inform teaching and identify gaps before they widen

Key Learning

Assessment for learning (AfL) is about gathering information to make teaching decisions in real-time. It answers the fundamental question: Do my students understand this well enough for me to move on?

  • Continuous, not occasional: AfL happens throughout every lesson, not just at the end of units. Every response is data about understanding.
  • Responsive teaching: The information you gather should change what you do next. If students don't understand, reteach before moving on.
  • Multiple methods: Use a range of techniques—questioning, mini-whiteboards, exit tickets, hinge questions—to get a clear picture of the whole class.
  • Focus on gaps: AfL helps identify the gap between where students are and where they need to be. Your job is to close that gap through targeted intervention.

Effective AfL means no student gets left behind because misconceptions were identified and addressed before they compounded.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Design a "hinge question" for an upcoming lesson—a single question that will reveal whether students are ready to move on. Plan what you'll do if more than 20% get it wrong. Use it and reflect on what it revealed.
Task 2
Implement exit tickets for one week. Ask one key question at the end of each lesson. Sort responses into "got it," "nearly there," and "need support." Use this to plan the start of your next lesson.
Task 3
Create a low-stakes retrieval quiz covering content from the last three weeks. Include 5-8 questions mixing recent and older material. Use the results to identify which topics need revisiting.

Module 8

Classroom Routines & Expectations

Building efficient routines that maximise learning time and reduce friction

Key Learning

Effective routines are the invisible infrastructure of great classrooms. When transitions, materials distribution, and common procedures run smoothly, precious learning time is protected and cognitive load for both teacher and students is reduced.

  • Routines must be taught: Don't assume students know how to enter silently, get equipment, or transition between activities. Explicitly teach, model, and practise every routine.
  • Invest time upfront: Spending 10 minutes teaching a routine in week one saves hours across the year. Front-load this investment—it pays off exponentially.
  • Consistent enforcement: A routine only works if it's consistently expected. Every time you let a routine slide, you have to rebuild it.
  • Review and refine: Periodically assess whether routines are working. If a transition consistently takes too long, reteach it with higher expectations.

The goal is automaticity—routines so embedded that they require no thought, freeing everyone to focus on learning.

Implementation Checklist

Rehearsal Tasks

Task 1
Map out every transition in a typical lesson. Time each one over three consecutive lessons. Identify the slowest transition and create a specific, teachable routine to speed it up. Reteach it explicitly to students.
Task 2
Create a "Do Now" routine for lesson starts—a task on the board that students begin immediately upon entry without teacher instruction. Practise this for a week, refining until students are working within 60 seconds of entering.
Task 3
Identify one routine that has been slipping recently. Plan a 5-minute reteach session: explain the routine, model it, have students practise it, then reset expectations. Notice the impact over the following week.
